Digital Habits of University Students:
Screen Time, Social Media Usage,
and Productivity Tool Preferences

Descriptive Survey Study — Academic Year 2025–2026

Course:Research Methods in Social Sciences Student:Max Herman Student ID:UEM-2026-10483 Date:May 19, 2026 Professor:Luis Muñoz Sample Size:n = 27 (convenience sample)

1. Introduction and Objective

The proliferation of smartphones and digital platforms has fundamentally transformed the daily routines of university students. According to recent reports by DataReportal (2025), individuals aged 18–24 represent the demographic cohort with the highest average daily screen time globally, spending upward of six hours per day on mobile devices. This pattern raises legitimate academic and public-health concerns, particularly with regard to its intersection with academic performance, sleep quality, and self-regulated study behavior.

Despite the abundance of global data, institution-level studies that capture the specific digital ecosystem of a single campus population remain rare. The present study attempts to address this gap by surveying students at a mid-sized Spanish private university.

Primary Objective: To understand how university students allocate their daily screen time across social media, academic tools, and entertainment, and to identify which digital habits they perceive as most harmful to their academic productivity.

Secondary Objectives

  • Map the dominant social media platforms consumed by the student population.
  • Determine the prevalence of self-reported productivity and study application usage.
  • Identify peak usage periods within the 24-hour cycle.
  • Quantify the subjective academic impact attributed to social media through a Likert-scale measure.

2. Survey Questionnaire

The questionnaire was designed to be concise and self-administered, comprising five closed-ended questions. The survey was distributed digitally via WhatsApp and social media platforms (Instagram, Facebook) to peers and friends, allowing respondents to complete it at their own pace. Questions were piloted informally with three classmates before distribution to confirm clarity of wording and appropriate response categories.

Q1 — Daily Smartphone Usage (hours per day)

How many hours per day do you typically spend on your smartphone?

  • A) Less than 2 hours
  • B) 2 – 4 hours
  • C) 4 – 6 hours
  • D) More than 6 hours

Q2 — Primary Social Media Platform

Which social media platform do you use most frequently?

  • A) Instagram
  • B) TikTok
  • C) WhatsApp
  • D) YouTube
  • E) X / Twitter

Q3 — Perceived Academic Impact (Likert Scale 1–5)

On a scale of 1 to 5, how much do you feel that social media negatively affects your academic performance?

  • 1 = Not at all
  • 2 = Slightly
  • 3 = Moderately
  • 4 = Considerably
  • 5 = Very much

Q4 — Productivity / Study App Usage

Do you regularly use any productivity or study application (e.g., Notion, Forest, Anki, Google Calendar, Pomodoro timers)?

  • A) Yes
  • B) No

Q5 — Peak Phone Usage Period

At what time of day do you use your phone the most?

  • A) Morning (06:00 – 11:59)
  • B) Afternoon (12:00 – 17:59)
  • C) Evening (18:00 – 21:59)
  • D) Night (22:00 – 02:00)

3.2 Formula (Finite Population Correction)

The Cochran (1977) formula for a finite population is used, which adjusts the infinite-population sample size downward when the population is known:

Formula: Z² × p × q × N n = ───────────────────────────────── e² × (N − 1) + Z² × p × q Substituting values: (1.96)² × 0.50 × 0.50 × 2400 n = ─────────────────────────────────────────────── (0.05)² × (2400 − 1) + (1.96)² × 0.50 × 0.50 Step 1 — Numerator: (1.96)² = 3.8416 3.8416 × 0.50 = 1.9208 1.9208 × 0.50 = 0.9604 0.9604 × 2400 = 2,304.96 Step 2 — Denominator: (0.05)² = 0.0025 0.0025 × 2399 = 5.9975 ← e² × (N−1) 3.8416 × 0.50 × 0.50 = 0.9604 ← Z² × p × q 5.9975 + 0.9604 = 6.9579 Step 3 — Result: n = 2,304.96 / 6.9579 n ≈ 331.3 → n = 332 (rounded up to the nearest whole number)
Interpretation: A statistically rigorous sample requires n = 332 respondents to achieve a 95% confidence level with a ±5% margin of error for a population of 2,400 students. This sample size is not feasible for a solo student project within the available time and resource constraints. Therefore, a convenience sample of n = 27 was obtained by distributing the questionnaire via WhatsApp and social media (Instagram, Facebook) to friends and peers during May 2026. While this sample does not meet the requirements for statistical generalizability, it is sufficient to satisfy the pedagogical objectives of this assignment and to illustrate the application of descriptive statistics methods. Results must be interpreted as exploratory and not extrapolated to the campus population.

7. Key Findings

1

High smartphone dependency is the norm: 74.1% of respondents report spending more than 4 hours per day on their smartphones (combining "4–6 hours" at 44.4% and "more than 6 hours" at 29.6%). Only 3.7% report spending fewer than 2 hours daily, suggesting that the low-usage segment is a clear minority within this student population.

2

TikTok and Instagram together dominate the social media landscape: These two platforms account for 66.7% of primary platform choices (TikTok 37.0%, Instagram 29.6%). WhatsApp, while ubiquitous in Spain, ranks third at 17.1%, likely because respondents interpreted the question as referring to content consumption rather than messaging utilities. X/Twitter has near-negligible presence (2.9%), consistent with its declining youth market share in Europe.

3

Respondents largely acknowledge social media's academic toll: The mean Likert score for perceived academic impact is x̄ = 3.48 / 5. A combined 55.5% of participants selected scores 4 ("Considerably") or 5 ("Very much"), while only 18.5% selected scores 1 or 2. This self-awareness is notable and may reflect a broader cultural moment of discourse around "doom-scrolling" and digital wellness in university-age populations.

4

A majority of students have adopted productivity applications: 59.3% of respondents indicate regular use of at least one productivity or study app. This is a surprisingly high adoption rate and may reflect self-selection bias (students engaged enough to complete a survey may also be more organized), or it may indicate genuine normalization of tools like Notion, Google Calendar, or Pomodoro timers within this cohort.

5

Night-time is the dominant phone usage window, with important implications for sleep: 40.7% of respondents identify the night (22:00–02:00) as their peak usage period — more than the Morning, Afternoon, and Evening categories combined (which collectively account for 59.3% but are spread across three time bands). This finding aligns with sleep-science literature linking late-night blue-light exposure and social media engagement to delayed sleep onset and reduced sleep quality among young adults (Twenge et al., 2018).

6

A coherent risk profile emerges: Cross-reading the five findings suggests a student who spends 4–6 hours on a smartphone, predominantly on TikTok, perceives significant damage to academic performance, uses night-time as their primary scroll window, and has responded to this self-awareness by adopting productivity tools — 59.3% in this sample. This profile is internally coherent and matches patterns reported in the broader literature on "compensatory self-regulation" in digitally native students.

8. Discussion

The data collected in this convenience sample paint a picture of moderate-to-high smartphone engagement combined with a degree of metacognitive awareness. The mean Likert score of 3.48 is particularly instructive: students are not passively consuming digital content without reflection — they recognize its academic costs. Yet this awareness has not translated into reduced screen time, as 74.1% remain above the 4-hour daily threshold.

This tension between awareness and behavior is consistent with what behavioral scientists call the intention-action gap. Students may intend to reduce their social media use, and may even download productivity apps to assist them (59.3% adoption rate), yet the structural design of platforms like TikTok — optimized for infinite scrolling and variable reward schedules — makes sustained behavioral change difficult without environmental intervention.

The dominance of night-time usage (40.7%) is particularly concerning from an academic standpoint. Evening and night smartphone use is strongly associated in the literature with later sleep onset and lower overall sleep duration, both of which independently predict poorer academic outcomes (Buxton et al., 2015). University administrations may therefore wish to consider digital wellness programming targeted specifically at this nocturnal usage window.

The decline of X/Twitter among this cohort (2.9%) merits brief comment. Following the platform's ownership change in late 2022 and subsequent user attrition, Spanish university-age users have largely migrated to visual-first short-form platforms, a shift corroborated by multiple European digital market reports published between 2023 and 2025.

9. Limitations

Sample size inadequacy: The convenience sample of n = 27 falls far short of the statistically required n = 332. Results cannot be generalized to the broader campus population with any specified confidence interval.
Non-probability sampling: Convenience sampling introduces selection bias. Participants who agreed to complete the survey via WhatsApp and social media may systematically differ from those who declined (e.g., in conscientiousness, academic engagement, or digital self-awareness). Digital distribution may also bias results toward more digitally active respondents.
Self-reporting bias: All measures rely on participants' retrospective self-assessment of their own habits, which is subject to social desirability bias and inaccurate recall. Objective screen-time data (e.g., from device analytics) were not collected.
Cross-sectional design: A single survey snapshot cannot establish causality between social media usage and academic performance. Longitudinal or quasi-experimental designs would be required for causal inference.
Single-item Likert measure (Q3): Academic impact was measured with a single item. Multi-item validated scales (e.g., the Bergen Social Media Addiction Scale or the DASS-21 academic subscale) would provide greater reliability and construct validity.
Context specificity: The sample was drawn from one private university in Madrid. Results may not transfer to public universities, universities in other regions, or students enrolled in different academic disciplines.

10. Conclusions

This exploratory study provides a preliminary descriptive portrait of the digital habits of a convenience sample of 27 university students. The central findings — high daily screen time concentrated in TikTok and Instagram, a perceived moderate-to-considerable academic impact (x̄ = 3.48 / 5), majority adoption of productivity tools, and peak usage occurring at night — form a coherent narrative that aligns with broader European and global trends documented in the digital wellness literature.

While the limitations of convenience sampling preclude generalization, the data serve their intended pedagogical function: to illustrate the end-to-end research process, from question design and sample size calculation through data collection, statistical description, and evidence-based interpretation. Future iterations of this research should employ probability sampling to achieve the target n = 332, integrate objective screen-time data, and adopt validated multi-item scales to improve measurement quality.
