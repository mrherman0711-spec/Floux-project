## PART 1: HOW TO THINK LIKE A CMO

### The CMO's First Questions (before any tactic)
Before writing a word, a senior CMO asks:
1. **Who exactly are we talking to?** (Not a demographic — a specific person with specific fears)
2. **Where are they in their awareness journey?** (Do they know the problem? Our solution? Our product?)
3. **What is the ONE job they're hiring this product to do?**
4. **What does inaction cost them?** (Loss framing, not gain framing)
5. **Why would they NOT buy?** (Objections first, benefits second)
6. **What category do we belong to — or should we CREATE?**
7. **What's the one thing we must be famous for?**

### CMO Rule: Message-Market Fit Before Product-Market Fit
You can't sell a great product with the wrong message. Get the message right first — find the words that make prospects say "that's exactly my problem." For Floux: the message "recuperamos las llamadas perdidas" is message-market fit. Every tactic must reinforce it.

### 1. EUGENE SCHWARTZ — 5 Levels of Customer Awareness
**Source:** Breakthrough Advertising (1966) — the most important copywriting book ever written.

**The 5 levels:**
- **Unaware** — doesn't know they have a problem. Copy: evocative images, emotional stories, no selling.
- **Problem Aware** — knows the pain but not the solution. Copy: name their pain exactly, create empathy.
- **Solution Aware** — knows solutions exist, hasn't chosen one. Copy: testimonials, case studies, comparison.
- **Product Aware** — knows your product, hasn't committed. Copy: offers, guarantees, remove friction.
- **Most Aware** — ready to buy. Copy: just give the price and the CTA.

**For Floux salon owners:** Most are Problem Aware ("I miss calls when I'm working") but NOT Solution Aware — they don't know WhatsApp AI booking exists. Your copy must move them from Problem → Solution Aware, not assume they're ready to buy.

**Tactic:** Open every ad/message at the Problem Aware level. Start with the pain ("Estás con un cliente. El teléfono suena. No puedes coger."), then introduce the solution.

### 2. ALEX HORMOZI — The Value Equation
**Source:** $100M Offers

**Formula:** Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)

**To increase value:**
- Raise Dream Outcome: Don't sell "missed call recovery" — sell "15 clientes más al mes"
- Raise Perceived Likelihood: Social proof, guarantees, case studies remove doubt
- Reduce Time Delay: "En 48 horas estás funcionando" — specificity kills hesitation
- Reduce Effort: "Tú no haces nada" — zero-effort positioning is Floux's strongest lever

**Grand Slam Offer components:**
1. Irresistible value proposition (outcome-led)
2. A starving crowd (salon owners with missed calls = starving crowd)
3. Premium pricing that signals quality (€97 is not expensive if framed vs €1,200 employee)
4. Guarantee that reverses all risk (30-day money back, or "si no recuperas un cliente, no cobras")

**CMO Application:** Floux's current offer is weak because it sells the feature (WhatsApp en llamada perdida), not the outcome (15 clientes al mes que antes perdías). Reframe everything around the dream outcome.

### 3. DAVID OGILVY — The Principles
**Source:** Ogilvy on Advertising, Confessions of an Advertising Man

**Core rules:**
- "The consumer is not a moron. She's your wife." — Respect intelligence. Never patronize.
- "5x as many people read the headline as the body copy." — If the headline fails, nothing else matters.
- "The more informative your advertising, the more persuasive it will be." — Facts > fluff. Specifics sell.
- "Every ad must contribute to the brand image." — Consistency across every touchpoint.
- "Hire people better than you and get out of their way." — (For building the agent team)
- Research first, always. "The more you know about a product, the better the idea."
- Make a clear promise. "Advertising which promises no benefit to the consumer doesn't sell."

**For Floux:** The headline "Recupera los clientes que pierdes mientras trabajas" follows Ogilvy's rule — it's a clear promise with a benefit. The stat "+15 clientes al mes" follows his specificity rule. What's missing: enough facts. Salon owners are skeptical — more facts = more trust.

### 4. CLAUDE HOPKINS — Scientific Advertising
**Source:** Scientific Advertising (1923) — still the most read book among direct response pros.

**Core principles:**
- "Advertising is salesmanship." Every word must earn its place by selling.
- Test everything. Hopkins invented A/B testing. Never assume — always measure.
- Headlines do 80% of the work. The rest is just closing.
- Offer free samples / trials to remove purchase risk. (Applies to Floux: free demo, free first month)
- Talk about benefits, not features. What does it mean for the buyer's life?
- Coupons and direct offers outperform brand-building for SMBs every time.
- "Give people a reason and they'll respond." Always answer: "Why should I act NOW?"

**For Floux:** The most powerful Hopkins tactic = **specific claim + reason to act now**. "Alta gratuita solo durante el lanzamiento" is exactly this. Specificity of the offer + urgency of the window = Hopkins-approved.

### 5. GARY HALBERT — Direct Response Copywriting
**Source:** The Boron Letters, The Gary Halbert Letter

**Core principles:**
- "The most important thing in any business is the starving crowd." Find people who desperately want what you sell before you write a word.
- Lead with the reader's self-interest, not your product's features.
- Write like you talk. Short sentences. Punchy. Real.
- "Reason-why copy" — always give a reason for the offer ("esto termina el viernes porque...").
- The P.S. in a letter/email is read more than the body. Use it for the most important thing.
- Create the "A-pile vs B-pile" test for every piece of copy — would the reader put this in the pile they actually read, or the trash?
- Use specificity to build credibility: "47 salones en Madrid" beats "muchos salones."

**For Floux:** Salon owners are the "starving crowd" — they feel the pain daily. Halbert says: you don't need great copy if you have the right crowd. Find the salons with the most missed calls first (prospecting), then even mediocre copy will convert.

### 6. RUSSELL BRUNSON — Hook, Story, Offer
**Source:** Traffic Secrets, Expert Secrets, DotCom Secrets

- **Story** — creates connection and belief. The story of how this product changed a life (yours or a customer's). Stories activate emotion: hope, curiosity, fear of missing out.
- **Offer** — the specific thing they get if they act. Must solve the problem the story introduced.

**If any funnel fails, it's always the Hook, Story, or Offer.**

**For Floux Hook options:**

### 9. DONALD MILLER — StoryBrand Framework (SB7)
**Source:** Building a StoryBrand

**The 7 parts:**
1. **Character (Customer)** — your customer is the hero, not your brand. Floux is not the hero. The salon owner is.
2. **Problem** — External (missed calls), Internal (anxiety, guilt, frustration), Philosophical ("ningún negocio debería perder clientes por estar trabajando")
3. **Guide (Your Brand)** — Floux as the wise guide with empathy + authority. Not a lecturer, a helper.
4. **Plan** — 3-step max. For Floux: 1) Llámanos, 2) Lo configuramos en 48h, 3) Recuperas clientes.
5. **Call to Action** — direct ("Reserva tu demo") or transitional ("Descarga el cálculo gratuito")
6. **Failure** — what happens if they don't act? The client calls the competitor. That competitor gains a loyal customer worth €1,750/year.
7. **Success** — the transformation: from stressed and missing calls → relaxed, fully booked, growing.

**CMO Application:** Most Floux copy positions Floux as the hero ("Floux hace X"). StoryBrand says flip it: the owner is the hero, Floux is the tool she uses to win. "Tú recuperas tus clientes. Nosotros te lo ponemos fácil."

### 10. APRIL DUNFORD — Obviously Awesome Positioning
**Source:** Obviously Awesome

**5-component positioning process (in order):**
1. **Competitive Alternatives** — What would the owner do WITHOUT Floux? (Miss the call, lose the client, do nothing)
2. **Unique Attributes** — What does Floux have that alternatives don't? (WhatsApp-native, sub-30s response, zero setup for owner, bilingual)

5. **Market Category** — What frame makes the value obvious? Not "software" (scary), not "chatbot" (cheap) — frame as "tu recepcionista de guardia" or better: "sistema de recuperación de clientes"

**Key insight:** Floux should NOT position in "SaaS" or "AI tool" category — those trigger skepticism in traditional SMBs. Position in "más clientes" category. "Conseguimos más clientes para tu salón" — that's the category she understands and wants.

### 11. JOANNA WIEBE — Conversion Copywriting & Voice of Customer
**Source:** Copyhackers

**Core method:**
- Find your best copy in your customers' own words. Read their reviews, WhatsApp messages, survey responses. Copy their language verbatim.
- "Make your voice, their voice." The copy that converts best sounds like the customer wrote it.

**Voice of Customer research for Floux:**

**Conversion copy hierarchy:** First, get the message right. Then optimize placement. Then test. Never test what you haven't gotten right conceptually first.

### 12. PEEP LAJA — CRO Methodology (CXL)
**Source:** CXL Institute

**Core framework:**
- "CRO is 80% research, 20% testing." Jump straight to A/B tests without research and you're wasting money.
- Conversion is driven by: **Motivation** (do they want it?), **Clarity** (do they understand it?), **Incentives** (what pushes them over the edge?)
- Conversion is blocked by: **Friction** (how hard is the action?) and **Anxiety** (what are they afraid of?)

**Heuristic analysis — evaluate every page for:**
1. Clarity — can a stranger understand what this is in 5 seconds?
2. Relevance — does this match what the visitor expected to see?
3. Value proposition — is it clear what they get and why it's better?
4. Friction — are there unnecessary steps, fields, or confusing choices?
5. Anxiety — what fears, doubts, or risks are unaddressed?
6. Distraction — are there elements pulling attention away from the main action?

**For Floux website:** Anxiety is the #1 conversion killer. The salon owner thinks: "Will this confuse my clients?" "Will I lose control?" "What if it breaks?" Address all three explicitly on the page.

### 13. ROBERT CIALDINI — 7 Principles of Influence
**Source:** Influence (1984), Pre-Suasion (2016)

**The 7 Principles:**
1. **Reciprocity** — Give first. A free "Cálculo de llamadas perdidas" PDF creates obligation before the sale.
2. **Commitment & Consistency** — Small yes leads to big yes. "¿Cuántas llamadas pierdes al día?" quiz before pricing = micro-commitment.

6. **Scarcity** — Loss aversion 2x gain seeking. "Solo aceptamos 1 salón por municipio" — if true, use it.
7. **Unity** — Shared identity. "Somos de aquí. Conocemos tu barrio." Local = unity trigger for Madrid salon owners.

### 14. KAHNEMAN — Loss Aversion & Prospect Theory
**Source:** Thinking, Fast and Slow; Prospect Theory (1979)

**Core insight:** Losses feel 2x more painful than equivalent gains feel good. "Don't lose €600/month" is twice as motivating as "earn €600/month."

**Marketing applications:**
- Frame offers around what clients LOSE by not acting, not what they gain by acting.
- "Cada llamada perdida son €50 que se van" > "Recupera €50 por llamada"
- "El 62% de clientes que no obtienen respuesta llaman a la competencia — y nunca vuelven" > "Consigue más clientes"
- Free trials exploit loss aversion: once owners USE Floux for 30 days, canceling feels like a loss.
- Scarcity + loss aversion = double effect: "Quedan 2 plazas en Majadahonda" creates fear of missing out.

**System 1 vs System 2:**
- System 1: fast, emotional, automatic. Triggered by images, stories, social proof.
- System 2: slow, rational, deliberate. Triggered by data, specs, comparisons.
- Salon owners buy on System 1 (emotion/fear/trust) and justify on System 2 (ROI, price, features). Design the page for System 1, provide System 2 reassurance below.

### 15. BJ FOGG — Behavior Design Model
**Source:** Tiny Habits, behaviormodel.org (Stanford)

**Formula:** Behavior happens when Motivation + Ability + Prompt converge simultaneously.

**For marketing:** If a prospect isn't converting, one of three things is wrong:
- **Motivation too low** — they don't want it enough (fix: better loss framing, stronger social proof)
- **Ability too low** — the action feels too hard (fix: remove friction, simplify CTA, shorten form)
- **Prompt missing or wrong timing** — they wanted it but weren't asked at the right moment (fix: better CTA placement, WhatsApp follow-up sequence)

**For Floux specifically:**
- Motivation: high (they feel the pain daily) — amplify with specifics
- Ability: CURRENTLY LOW — "setup" and "WhatsApp API" feel technical and scary — fix with "en 48 horas está funcionando, tú no haces nada"
- Prompt: unclear on website — there are multiple CTAs competing. One dominant CTA per page.

### 16. JOBS TO BE DONE — Clayton Christensen
**Source:** Competing Against Luck, Harvard Business Review

**Core insight:** People don't buy products — they "hire" them to do a job in their life. The real job is functional + emotional + social.

**The salon owner's jobs:**
- **Functional job:** "Help me not lose clients when I'm busy with someone"
- **Emotional job:** "Help me feel less stressed and in control"
- **Social job:** "Help me look like a modern, professional salon owner without having to become technical"

**Why this matters:** Marketing that speaks to the emotional and social job converts better than marketing that speaks to the functional job. "Tus clientas verán que siempre estás disponible" (social job) > "recupera llamadas perdidas" (functional job).

**The milkshake insight:** People often hire products for unexpected reasons. Ask: in what unexpected situations might a salon owner hire Floux? (Answer: when she's on vacation, when she's sick, when she's with an important VIP client and can't look at the phone.)

### 18. JONAH BERGER — STEPPS (Word of Mouth Engineering)
**Source:** Contagious: Why Things Catch On (Wharton)

**The 6 STEPPS drivers of virality:**
1. **Social Currency** — sharing makes the sharer look good. "Mi salón nunca pierde una llamada" is social currency for a proud owner.
2. **Triggers** — top of mind = tip of tongue. Every missed call is a trigger to think about Floux. Use "llamada perdida" as the trigger phrase in all copy.
3. **Emotion** — high-arousal emotions (awe, anger, anxiety) drive sharing. The anxiety of losing clients to competitors is the emotion to trigger.
4. **Public** — visible = viral. A WhatsApp response to a client is visible to the client. If it's warm and professional, the client mentions it.
5. **Practical Value** — useful information gets shared. A free "calculadora de llamadas perdidas" gets shared in salon owner WhatsApp groups.
6. **Stories** — narratives are remembered and retold. Max's story of walking Madrid streets needs to be shareable.

**Local B2B word-of-mouth:** Salon owners talk to each other constantly — same suppliers, same fairs, same neighborhoods. One satisfied Floux client in Majadahonda tells 5 competitors. Design for this: ask every client for a referral. Give them the exact words to use.

### 19. REFORGE / BRIAN BALFOUR — Growth Loops
**Source:** Reforge Advanced Growth Strategy

**Loops > Funnels:**
- A funnel is linear: Awareness → Interest → Decision → Action. It ends.
- A growth loop is circular: each output becomes the next input. It compounds.

**Floux's potential growth loops:**
- **Word-of-mouth loop:** Client recovers →  tells other salon owner → new client sign-up → repeats
- **Review loop:** Floux sends Google review requests → more 5-star reviews → better Google ranking → more inbound leads
- **Referral loop:** Happy client refers colleague → colleague signs up → both get benefit → both refer

**CMO Application:** Stop optimizing just the acquisition funnel. Design the retention loop first — a client who stays 12 months and refers 2 others is worth 10x a client who churns at month 2.

### 21. DAN ARIELY — Predictably Irrational (Pricing Psychology)
**Source:** Predictably Irrational

**The Decoy Effect:** Add a third option that makes the middle option look like a steal. Floux's 3-plan structure (Básico €47, Agente €97, Completo €147) is correct — Básico is the decoy that makes Agente look like the smart choice.

**Anchoring:** The first number seen anchors all subsequent judgments. Show Completo (€147) first, then Agente (€97) looks like a bargain. Currently showing Básico first — consider reordering.

**The "FREE" effect:** Free is irrational — people dramatically overvalue free. "Alta gratuita" is not just zero cost — it's psychologically more powerful than a 50% discount.

**Pain of paying:** Subscriptions reduce pain of paying because the payment feels distant. Emphasize the monthly low cost ("menos de €3.25 al día") to minimize the psychological pain.

### 24. AARON ROSS — Predictable Revenue (Outbound System)
**Source:** Predictable Revenue (Salesforce)

**The 3 types of leads:**
1. **Seeds** — word of mouth, organic. Slowest but highest quality. Invest in making existing clients evangelists.
2. **Nets** — inbound marketing (content, SEO, social). Medium speed. Build over time.
3. **Spears** — targeted outbound. Fastest for early stage. Cold outreach to specific salons.

**The Cold Email Formula:**
- Ultra-short (5 sentences max)
- One very specific claim ("ayudamos a salones en Majadahonda a recuperar 15 clientes al mes")
- One question (not a pitch)
- No attachments, no PDFs, no links in first email
- Example: "Hola [nombre], vi tu salón en Google Maps y me surgió una pregunta — ¿cuántas llamadas pierdes por semana mientras estás con clientas?"

**For Floux at launch:** Spears first (direct outreach to specific salons), Seeds second (referral program), Nets third (SEO content, Instagram).

### 30. LUXURY POSITIONING FOR LOCAL B2B
**Synthesized from multiple sources**

**Why a luxury aesthetic sells to skeptical traditional owners:**
- It signals quality and permanence ("this isn't a fly-by-night startup")
- It triggers "aspirational self-concept" — the owner imagines her salon as luxurious/premium
- It reduces price sensitivity — luxury buyers don't haggle
- It differentiates from every other "tech" pitch she's seen (which all look the same: blue, white, "digital")

**The luxury brand rules for Floux:**
1. Never look desperate. Scarcity > availability.
2. Show, don't tell. Don't say "we're premium" — look premium.
3. The brand speaks before the product. First impression = website, Instagram, pitch deck. All must feel like Four Seasons.
4. Price is a quality signal. Never apologize for €97.
5. Exclusivity creates desire. "Solo trabajamos con salones seleccionados en Madrid."

## PART 3: FRAMEWORKS FOR CREATIVE IDEATION

### The "10 Headlines" Exercise (Ogilvy / Hopkins)
Before choosing a headline, write 20 options. The first 5 are obvious. Options 6-10 are good. Options 11-20 contain the breakthrough. Never use your first headline.

### The "Enemy" Framework (Category Design / StoryBrand)
Every great brand has a villain. For Floux:
- The villain is NOT competitors (Booksy, Treatwell)
- The villain is: **the missed call itself** — the silent, invisible drain on every salon
- Secondary villain: the old way of doing things ("contestador automático," "llamaré cuando pueda")

### The "Before/After/Bridge" Structure (Brunson / Hopkins)
- **Before:** "Estás cortando el pelo. Suena el teléfono. No puedes coger. Ese cliente llama a la competencia."
- **After:** "Ese mismo cliente recibe un WhatsApp en 30 segundos. Queda con cita. Tú ni te enteraste hasta que te llegó el resumen."
- **Bridge:** "Eso es Floux."

### The "So What?" Ladder (Dunford / Hormozi)
Take any feature and ask "so what?" until you reach the emotional outcome:
- "Floux envía WhatsApps" → so what? → "el cliente recibe respuesta" → so what? → "el cliente no llama a otro salón" → so what? → "no pierdes ese cliente" → so what? → "son €50 que se quedan en tu caja" → so what? → "son €600 al mes que antes perdías sin saberlo"

The bottom of the ladder is the copy. The top is the feature list nobody cares about.

### The "Specificity = Credibility" Rule (Halbert / Ogilvy / Hopkins)
- "Muchos salones" → weak
- "15 salones en Madrid" → better
- "12 salones en Majadahonda, Pozuelo y Las Rozas" → strong
- "El salón de Carmen en Calle Mayor de Majadahonda recupera 18 clientes al mes" → irresistible

The more specific, the more believable.

## PART 4: THE FLOUX ICP DEEP PROFILE

### Who is the buyer (beyond demographics)
- **Age:** 45-60 female, salon owner for 10-20 years
- **Identity:** Her craft is her identity. She's proud of what she does with her hands.
- **Technology relationship:** Skeptical. She's been sold "the next big thing" before and it complicated her life.
- **Primary fear:** Losing control. Any product that makes her feel less in control of her business will fail.
- **Secondary fear:** Looking foolish. She doesn't want to try something and have it fail in front of her clients.
- **What she's proud of:** Her regulars. Her reputation. Her "clientas de toda la vida."
- **What makes her angry:** Being talked down to. Tech bros who don't understand her world. Corporate language.
- **What she secretly wants:** To be more modern without having to become modern. To have the result without the effort.
- **Who she trusts:** Other salon owners. Her supplier reps. Local business owners she knows personally.
- **Decision-making style:** Slow. Needs social proof. Needs to see it work. Needs to trust the person selling it.

### The 3 objections and how to handle them (Kennedy / Cialdini)
1. **"Lo pienso"** = "I don't trust this enough yet." → Don't push. Provide more social proof + case study + follow up in 3 days.
2. **"No tengo tiempo para aprenderlo"** = Fogg's Ability barrier. → "Tú no aprendes nada. Nosotros lo configuramos todo en 48h."
3. **"Es caro"** = Not a price objection — a value objection. She doesn't see the ROI yet. → "¿Cuántas llamadas pierdes al día? A €50 por cliente, ¿cuánto es eso al mes? Floux cuesta €97."

## PART 5: WAVE 0 — CREATIVE IDEATION PROTOCOL

Before auditing anything, the creative agent must:

1. **Read the ICP profile** (Part 4 above)
2. **Identify the current awareness level** of the target audience (Schwartz — Part 2, #1)
3. **Apply the Jobs To Be Done lens** — what is the owner really hiring this for? (Functional + Emotional + Social job)
4. **Run the StoryBrand check** — who is the hero? Who is the guide? What's the villain?
5. **Generate 10 headline options** using the "10 Headlines" exercise — never use the first
6. **Apply the "So What?" ladder** to every main feature
7. **Check for Purple Cow** — is there anything genuinely remarkable? What would make a salon owner tell a friend?
8. **Apply STEPPS** — does this content have Social Currency, a Trigger, Emotion, Public visibility, Practical Value, and a Story?
9. **Loss frame check** — is the main message framed as avoiding a loss or achieving a gain? (Loss is 2x more powerful)
10. **Specificity audit** — replace every vague claim with the most specific version possible
